Cybernetic Dream

In many ways, this whole experience has felt like a dream. First of all, it has been marked by the pandemic lockdown. I have spent a lot of time alone, in a new country, alterning between the Canberra's greenness and my computer screen. Everything seems new and often I feel like I stepped out from reality into a dimension where my sole purpose is to learn.

One of the key learning experiences has been one of my two courses at the 3Ai Institue, the Fundamentals of a New Science Lab, which we called Build. Here, I have been guided by an amazing group of tutors as I develop my skills towards becoming a practicioner of a new kind of engineering, which by the way, is being created as it is being taught. What is this new branch of engineering, you ask? Nobody knows yet, but so far, my best take at a one sentence summary is: "things are always more complicated than you think". Everything exists across different domains and have different dimensions. We focus on technological systems and particularly a subset called cyberphysical ones. These are systems of networked computers that sense and control physical processes, with feedback loops between them. Self driving cars, smart electrical grids and ecological monitoring sort of stuff. But they are not only made of wires and circuits, they interact with systems across technical, social, political, ecological and ethical espectrums, to name a few.

How do we unpack this complexity? Fortunately, we are not the first ones to ask this question. A man named Norbert Wiener, a fellow mexican Arturo Ronseblueth and many others got together in the 40's and came up with a few ideas. What resulted became the foundation of what they called cybernetics. You have probably heard the word, and you probably understand it as something very different from the actual definition. Cybernetics aims to study the world a set of interconnected complex systems, with feedback loops that give rise to the emergence of complex behavior. These systems are everywhere: our physiology, water processes in the biosphere or the internet are all examples of cybernetic processes. They exist anywhere where there is a form of control and feedback machanism. The rise of artificial intelligence opens the door to a completely new class of system: autonomous digital computers that control the physical world around us: cities, ecosystems and our bodies. These are the systems that we focus on at 3Ai.

A good way to study cyberphysical systems is to build them. But we start small, by developing skills. Tutors guide us through fortnightly projects while we are also given freedom to direct our own journey. Therefore, every student has a different one. This is mine.

Musical experiments

One type of system that I am very interested in is autonomous music generation. We can imagine a system that autonomously composes music, optimizing towards personalized user preferences. For that, it a way to acquire user's feedback. At first it can be an intentional signal, like a button, but in the future, it could sense the user's brains dopamine levels. In both cases, the user and the system influence each other in an intricate loop that exists across domains like biology, mathematics, culture, power and data.

Browser Experiments

This led me to tinker around with javascript more and explore its possibilities for fun interactions in the browser. I created a few web experiments that explore some cybernetic concept, like randomness, agency, initial conditions, feedback loops and entropy. Try the experiments below!

Color experiment

Concepts: randomness, agency, initial conditions

If you tap the screen or over move your mouse around, you will see that the color changes accordingly. Try to find your favorite.

You will start to notice patterns, but you will also notice that your options are limited. One of the three components that determine a color was set randomly and remains fixed. The other two can be manipulated with the X and Y coordinates of your screen tap or cursor.

But wait! You can roll the dice again to change your fixed variable for a new random number. If you want to find your favorite color, when will you decide to reroll vs. stay with your options? How do you navigate between randomness and agency?

I then came across p5.js, a great tool for creating visual art in the browser, developed by Lauren McCarthy. My main learning resource was THE AMAZING p5.js course by Daniel Shiffman. Seriously, I cannot stress enough how good his YouTube Channel is, The Coding Train. P5.js was my main resource is what I used for my final project a musical automata, which is a complete working prototype of a music generating system in the browser that receives user feedback. Through the last month, as I learned abiut cybernetics and complex system, I became fascinated by cellular automata: a simple mathematical system where cells in a grid interact with each other according to some simple initial rules, at sequential time steps. Some people believe that the Universe itself is a cellular automata, and that you could simulate any system with them. True or not, cellular automata exhibit interesting cybernetic properties. Complexity emerges from simple initial conditions. Feedback loops are formed, even though they are not explicityely programmed. If cellular automata are visualized, they can exhibit beautiful visual patterns. So, for my prototype, I wondered if I could use a cellular automata to generate music and visual art, starting from initial user defined conditions and rules of interaction, right in the browser. The user could change conditions at any point, or rules, and the behavior of the automaton would change. You can play with the project here or go to directly to https://musical-automata.glitch.com

The arrangement of the galleries is always the same: Twenty bookshelves, five to each side, line four of the hexagon's six sides; the height of the bookshelves, floor to ceiling, is hardly greater than the height of a normal librarian. To the left and right of the vestibule are two tiny compartments. One is for sleeping, upright; the other, for satisfying one's physical necessities. Through this space, too, there passes a spiral staircase, which winds upward and downward into the remotest distance. In the vestibule there is a mirror, which faithfully duplicates appearances. Men often infer from this mirror that the Library is not infinite-if it were, what need would there be for that illusory replication?
